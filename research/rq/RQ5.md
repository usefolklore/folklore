## RQ5 — Shared inference as submodular coverage: compounding is plausible and selective, but its *measurability across users* — and the in-repo precision numbers the argument leans on — are the weak links

**Answer.** The literature gives strong indirect support for the *mechanism* Folklore bets on — that selectively shared, relevant prior work compounds with diminishing returns and a coverage-style structure — and one source (S-RAG) supplies the exact mathematical object (monotone submodular maximization, `(1-1/e)` guarantee) that Folklore hypothesizes governs its compounding. What no source supplies is a clean, multi-user, longitudinal *measurement* that shared inference reliably compounds across a real population. And — important caveat the original note got wrong — Folklore's own *real-corpus* evidence is much weaker than the precision figures the note quoted: the headline compounding and separation numbers are simulator-derived, while the repo's measured real-geometry admission gate tops out at AUC 0.78 (with the shipped composite at 0.52). So the honest verdict is: the compounding hypothesis is well-motivated and partially supported at the component level *in simulation*; the across-users-at-scale reliability claim remains an open empirical question that Folklore's 100-peer pilot is designed to answer but has not yet answered.

**What the literature shows.**

*S-RAG / Submodular-RAG* (OpenReview `gtcOku1v2s`, "Optimal Document Selection in RAG via Combinatorial Optimization: A Theoretical Framework," ACL ARR 2026 January) is the most load-bearing source. It frames document selection as monotone submodular maximization under a token-budget knapsack constraint over query-relevant concepts, and proves a `(1-1/e)` approximation guarantee for the surrogate objective. Empirically it beats Top-k and MMR on EM, BERTScore/ROUGE, and LLM-as-judge across Natural Questions, ELI5, and HotpotQA, with the largest gains on multi-hop questions. Its diagnosis of naive top-k — that it wastes budget on redundant evidence and fails to cover complementary facts — is precisely the diminishing-returns/coverage geometry Folklore conjectures for accumulated peer outputs. Caveat for transfer: S-RAG optimizes selection *within a single query's context window over a curated corpus*, not *temporal accumulation across many users' organic memory* — so it supplies the mathematical object, it does not confirm Folklore's setting.

*SUPER* (arXiv 2311.00865; "Selectively Sharing Experiences Improves Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning," Gerstgrasser, Danino, Keren, NeurIPS 2023) is the strongest evidence for the *selective* qualifier. In multi-agent RL, sharing only a small number of highly relevant transitions outperforms both no-sharing and full-sharing, robustly across hyperparameters and DQN variants. This is a direct empirical instance of the claim that reuse value is governed by *relevance/precision*, not by raw volume of shared material — agreeing with S-RAG's anti-redundancy result from a different subfield.

*Collaborative Memory* (arXiv 2505.18279; "Multi-User Memory Sharing in LLM Agents with Dynamic Access Control," Rezazadeh et al.) supplies the multi-user sharing architecture: a two-tier private/shared memory with immutable provenance (contributing agents, accessed resources, timestamps) and asymmetric, time-varying read/write policies, claiming "provable adherence" to those policies. It confirms cross-user knowledge sharing with access control is a tractable design. (The paper does report its own experiments; the abstract-level extraction surfaced no numbers, and in any case none of its experiments measure *cross-user temporal compounding* — so it bears on feasibility and access-control, not on the compounding integral.)

*Federated RAG: A Systematic Mapping Study* (ACL Findings-EMNLP 2025.388; Chakraborty, Dahal, Gupta) is a meta-source: the first systematic mapping of Federated RAG (2020–2025). It names the open problems — privacy-preserving retrieval, cross-client heterogeneity, and **evaluation limitations** — without reporting numbers. Its flagging of evaluation as an unsolved field-wide gap is a mild *conflict* with Folklore's "reliably & measurably" framing: the community lacks agreed instruments for measuring federated reuse quality, which is exactly the claim Folklore needs to substantiate.

**How it maps to Folklore.** The literature is **consistent with** Folklore's core structural hypothesis without confirming it: S-RAG independently arrives at monotone submodular coverage with the same `(1-1/e)` greedy bound Folklore posits, and both S-RAG and SUPER support the corollary that reliability is bounded by retrieval *precision/relevance*, not by network size. The important correction is on Folklore's *own* precision substrate. The note previously cited "measured separation between paraphrase and spurious hits (cos median 0.841 vs 0.097, AUC 0.999, σ≈0.033)" — **those figures are not in the repo** and reflect the synthetic simulator's clean Gaussian clusters, not measured real geometry. Folklore's actual measured real-corpus separation is AUC **0.78** for the energy/Hopfield admission gate (36 in-corpus + 22 out-of-corpus; 57% true-admit at the Youden point) and AUC **0.52** with 0% web-deflection for the *shipped* `satisfaction ≥ 0.85` composite (`src/domain/energy-gate.ts`). Likewise, "false-admit ~0–1%" is the *simulator* bound (`tests/compounding-graded.test.ts` asserts ≤2% on synthetic σ=0.1 geometry); the real-corpus (SciFact) bound it locks is ≤15%. So the precision that any submodular-coverage argument needs is demonstrated cleanly only in simulation; on real geometry it is materially weaker and not yet at a level that certifies reliable per-hit precision. Collaborative Memory **extends** Folklore by formalizing provenance + asymmetric policy as a richer version of Folklore's single `private` flag and CRDT replication, suggesting a maturation path. The Federated RAG study **challenges** the "measurably" half of RQ5: the field lacks standard evaluation for exactly this, so Folklore cannot lean on external benchmarks — it must produce its own longitudinal evidence.

Crucially, every source measures a *single-shot or single-training-run* quantity (better retrieval, better sample efficiency, safe sharing). None measures *temporal compounding across many users* — the integral over time of reuse value as a population grows. Folklore's own compounding numbers (e.g. P=64: cooperative 90.2% vs isolated 18.4% hit, 9.1× fewer web trips) are likewise explicitly **SIMULATOR** results (`bench-compounding.mjs`); the real-corpus compounding bench exists but is not the headline. That temporal-cross-user gap is what the pilot targets, and the literature neither confirms nor refutes it. Pre-pilot, the honest label is "promising thesis + solid infra, validated mainly in simulation," not "validated."

**Open questions / the falsifier.** Sharpest unresolved question: does the marginal value of the *n*-th shared inference actually decay like a submodular coverage curve in a live heterogeneous population, or does cross-user *distribution shift* (everyone querying different things) keep marginal value flat or even negative once stale/spurious hits accumulate? The falsifier is concrete and Folklore can run it in the 100-peer 30-day pilot: plot net task-quality lift versus cumulative shared-node count per user. Two distinct failure modes, each refuting a different half of the hypothesis: (a) if the curve is **linear or non-saturating** (no diminishing returns), the submodular-coverage / `(1-1/e)` model borrowed from S-RAG does not transfer from curated corpora to organically federated peer memory; (b) if lift goes **flat or negative** as the graph grows — recall improves but retrieval precision degrades and false-admits rise — then compounding is not *reliable* across users. The honest precision baseline for that second test is the repo's *measured real-geometry* number (energy-gate AUC 0.78; SciFact false-admit ≤15%), **not** the ≤1% simulator figure; if live false-admits drift toward or past the SciFact bound as the graph grows, that is the degradation signal. Either observation refutes the central RQ5 hypothesis.

*Source note: S-RAG and SUPER returned concrete methods/metrics; Collaborative Memory and the Federated RAG mapping study returned abstract-level content only (no numbers), so claims from those two are scoped to feasibility/field-structure, not measured compounding. All in-repo precision/compounding figures above are flagged as simulator vs real where they differ; the 0.841/0.097/0.999/0.033 geometry figures could not be located in the repo and have been removed.*
