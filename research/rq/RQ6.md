## RQ6 — Benchmarks for testing the reuse-layer assumptions: retrieval generalization, memory competence, and the missing joint metric

**Answer.** Mature, well-cited benchmarks already exist for the *component* assumptions Folklore depends on — embedding/retrieval quality (MTEB, BEIR) and long-term memory recall/reasoning (LongMemEval, LoCoMo, MemoryAgentBench). They support two design choices in Folklore's harness (BEIR datasets, recall metrics, memory adapters) and one uncomfortable truth: no single embedder or retriever generalizes across tasks, so a fixed MiniLM-L6 index is a known weak point. But none of these benchmarks measures the thing Folklore actually claims — *cross-peer, demand-shaped reuse* jointly scored on quality, savings, and trust. That gap is real and is the sharpest place to falsify the thesis.

**What the literature shows.** BEIR (arXiv:2104.08663, Thakur et al., NeurIPS 2021 D&B) assembles 18 datasets across diverse retrieval tasks and evaluates ten retrieval systems spanning five architecture classes (lexical, sparse, dense, late-interaction, re-ranking). Its headline finding is adversarial to naive dense retrieval: BM25 is a robust baseline; re-ranking and late-interaction models give the best *zero-shot* average but at high compute cost; dense models are cheaper yet often underperform and generalize poorly out-of-distribution. MTEB (arXiv:2210.07316, Muennighoff et al.) generalizes this across 8 task types, 58 datasets, 112 languages, and 33 benchmarked models, reaching a compatible conclusion stated plainly: no single embedding method dominates across all tasks, and strong STS performance does not transfer to other uses. These two agree: retrieval quality is task- and domain-contingent, not a solved constant.

On the memory side, LongMemEval (arXiv:2410.10813, Wu et al., ICLR 2025) curates 500 questions over long chat histories testing five abilities — information extraction, multi-session reasoning, temporal reasoning, knowledge updates, and abstention — and reports roughly a 30% accuracy drop for commercial assistants and long-context LLMs on sustained-interaction recall; the paper's unified framework, decomposing memory design across indexing, retrieval, and reading stages, *substantially improves* recall but is not claimed to fully recover the drop. LoCoMo (arXiv:2402.17753, Maharana et al.) pushes context length further — up to 35 sessions, ~300 turns, ~9K tokens average — across question answering, event summarization, and multimodal dialogue generation, finding that both long-context and RAG approaches still substantially lag humans. MemoryAgentBench (arXiv:2507.05257, Hu et al.) reframes memory as four competencies — accurate retrieval, test-time learning, long-range understanding, and selective forgetting — evaluated in *incremental multi-turn* form, and reports that no current method masters all four. The memory papers agree with each other and extend BEIR/MTEB: retrieval alone is necessary but not sufficient; temporal updates, abstention, and forgetting are distinct, separately-failing axes.

**How it maps to Folklore.** The literature *confirms* several harness choices. Folklore wiring BEIR SciFact/NFCorpus/FiQA and scoring against gold qrels (its BEIR runs report NDCG@10 / MAP@10 / Recall@10; its memory adapters report `recall_any@K`) is the BEIR-sanctioned protocol, and using real LongMemEval/LoCoMo adapters places Folklore on the recognized memory benchmarks rather than bespoke proxies. Folklore's metric discipline — recall scored against gold qrels rather than against its own cache gate, attributable-ASR (clean-correct → poison-flip), and explicit simulator-vs-measured labelling — is the kind of falsify-your-own-thesis rigor these papers model, and it guards against the self-confirming error of scoring a cache against the cache. The literature also *challenges* Folklore: MTEB/BEIR's "no universal embedder" result means a single frozen MiniLM-L6 384d index will be systematically weak on some domains and OOD queries, so reuse hit-rate is bounded by an embedder the benchmarks say cannot be optimal everywhere (consistent with the repo's own finding that a cross-encoder reranker gave zero lift on LongMemEval-S). MemoryAgentBench's *selective forgetting* and LongMemEval's *knowledge updates / abstention* axes directly stress Folklore's freshness rule (the 7-day staleness window and `age_days` aging): a stale-but-confident peer hit is precisely the knowledge-update failure those benchmarks isolate, and Folklore currently has no benchmark scoring whether its aging policy abstains correctly. Finally, every cited benchmark is single-node and single-corpus; none measures cross-peer demand-shaped reuse, network savings, or trust under poisoning jointly — confirming the stated gap.

**Open questions / the falsifier.** The sharpest unresolved question: does demand-shaped, *federated* reuse beat a single-node baseline (the repo's named A/B baseline, vCache, arXiv:2502.03771) once you hold quality constant and price in trust? Note the dependency split this rests on: Folklore's retrieval-quality numbers (`recall_any@K`, NDCG@10) are *measured on real corpora*, but its central compounding result — web-fallback collapsing 17% → 1% as the network warms — is a *simulator number* under a boolean "does peer N hold doc D" abstraction that omits per-peer retrieval quality (the repo labels this v1; v2 plugs real retrieval in). The joint quality×savings×trust metric does not yet exist publicly, so the central claim is under-instrumented. Three falsifiers: (1) Realism — if the 17%→1% compounding does not survive replacing the boolean peer-holds-doc abstraction with real, lossy per-peer retrieval, the compounding thesis is a simulator artifact. (2) Frontier — on a held-out BEIR/LongMemEval split, if Folklore's reuse layer raises savings only by accepting hits that lower qrels recall versus vCache at matched attributable-ASR, then "demand-shaped reuse improves the frontier" is false; it merely trades along it. (3) Embedder confound — following MTEB, if swapping the embedder reorders Folklore's reuse hit-rate as much as the federation itself does, the measured gains are an embedder artifact, not evidence for the P2P-reuse hypothesis.
