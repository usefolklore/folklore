## RQ2 — Admission gates detect distribution membership, not answerability or staleness

**Answer.** The literature converges on a finding that Folklore's own measurements already foreshadow: a single similarity/energy score computed at *admission time* can separate in-distribution from out-of-distribution queries, but it cannot reliably separate *answerable from unanswerable*, nor *fresh from stale*, nor *correct-context from misleading-context*. Detecting bad reuse is therefore not one problem but at least three (out-of-knowledge, stale, and conflict-induced misuse), and the evidence says they require different mechanisms operating at different points in the pipeline — not one threshold.

**What the literature shows.** The most direct match is Li et al., *Do You Know What You Are Talking About? Characterizing Query-Knowledge Relevance for Reliable RAG* (arXiv 2410.08320). It explicitly splits the reliability failure into the same two axes Folklore cares about — *out-of-knowledge queries* (the corpus does not cover the query) and *stale knowledge* (the corpus is outdated) — and proposes goodness-of-fit statistical tests for per-query *online* detection plus an *offline* distribution-shift test over query *collections*. It reports evaluation on eight QA datasets. The abstract discloses no AUROC/threshold figures, so the strength of separation is unverified from my read — treat the two-axis framing as confirmed but the quantitative claims as not established.

*AbstentionBench* (arXiv 2506.09038, Kirichenko et al.) supplies the sharpest cautionary number I could verify: across 20 datasets and 20 frontier LLMs, reasoning fine-tuning *degrades* abstention on unanswerable questions by 24% on average, and scaling model size alone does not fix it; a crafted system prompt helps somewhat but the core inability to reason about uncertainty persists. Its failure taxonomy explicitly includes *outdated information* and *false premises* (alongside underspecification, unknown answers, and subjective questions) — i.e., exactly the stale/misleading-context cases. *Uncertainty-Based Abstention in LLMs* (arXiv 2404.10960, Tomani et al.) is the optimistic counterpoint: uncertainty-driven abstention buys +2–8% correctness, avoids ~50% of hallucinations on unanswerable questions, and raises safety from 70% up to 99%. But this paper's scope is *general* LLM abstention, not retrieved/noisy RAG context, so its transfer to RAG staleness is an assumption, not a demonstrated result.

The two papers that most *challenge* a gate-only design: *Astute RAG* (arXiv 2410.07176, Wang et al., ACL 2025) frames imperfect retrieval as "inevitable, common, and harmful" and, rather than detect-and-deny, consolidates internal and external knowledge with *source-awareness*, claiming to be the only RAG method matching or surpassing no-RAG use under the worst case where retrieved docs mislead (specific datasets and point-gain tables not verifiable from the abstract). And *ReDeEP* (OpenReview ztzZDzgfrh, Sun et al., ICLR 2025 Spotlight) is the deepest challenge: hallucination occurs *even with accurate and relevant retrieved content*, because Knowledge FFNs overweight parametric knowledge while Copying Heads fail to carry the external context (its AARF variant mitigates by modulating those components). If correct context can still be ignored, then no admission-time relevance score is sufficient — the failure lives in generation, not retrieval. These two conflict in remedy (consolidate vs. mechanistically re-weight) but agree that admission filtering alone is the wrong altitude.

**How it maps to Folklore.** The query-knowledge-relevance two-axis framing (2410.08320) **confirms** Folklore's decomposition: the energy-OOD admission gate is the analog of their online GoF test, and Folklore's freshness multiplier `phi = FRESH_FLOOR + (1-FRESH_FLOOR)·0.5^(age/7)` is the stale-knowledge axis they detect statistically. Folklore's measured result is internally consistent with the abstention literature: the energy gate scores AUC 0.78 on a *constructed* in/out-of-distribution split (57% true-admit / 0% false-admit at fitted tau, vs AUC 0.52 for the old composite-satisfaction score), but on *real* queries deny-gate calibration was a clean null at AUC 0.52, with the conclusion "answerability != lexical overlap." AbstentionBench (2506.09038) and ReDeEP (ztzZDzgfrh) **explain** that null rather than excuse it: answerability and context-misuse are not lexical/distributional properties, so a similarity-energy statistic *should* fail on them — the 0.52 is the predicted outcome, not a bug. The literature therefore **extends** Folklore in one concrete direction: Astute RAG suggests that where the gate cannot deny safely (the real-query regime), source-aware *consolidation* at generation time may recover worst-case accuracy that a binary deny cannot — though this is a research direction, not a measured Folklore result.

**Open questions / the falsifier.** The energy gate's AUC 0.78 was measured on a constructed OOD split — distribution membership, not answerability. RQ2's real danger is the *on-distribution but stale/misleading* hit: semantically near the query, high admit-score, yet factually outdated. ReDeEP predicts such high-similarity stale context will be *over-trusted*, and the real-query null (AUC 0.52) hints the gate gives no protection here. The sharp falsifier: take cache hits that the energy gate *admits* with high confidence, partition them by ground-truth staleness/correctness, and measure downstream answer error. If admitted high-confidence stale hits carry the *same or higher* error rate as rejected ones, the gate provides zero protection against exactly the failure RQ2 names — and the freshness multiplier, still uncalibrated on real traffic, becomes the only line of defense, untested. The hypothesis that admission-time scoring catches misleading reuse dies the moment that error-rate-by-admission-confidence curve is flat.
