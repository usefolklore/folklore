## RQ4 — Per-hit speedups are large but conditional; the honest saving is speedup × hit-rate × (1 − error cost)

**Answer.** The literature reports large *conditional* savings, but the headline numbers are heterogeneous metrics, not a common unit: GPTCache claims 2–10× faster *responses on a hit*, Prompt Cache 8× (GPU) to 60× (CPU) lower *time-to-first-token*, and SGLang up to 6.4× *throughput*. Each is a *per-hit* (or per-structured-call) multiplier, contingent on a reuse event actually occurring and being correct. None of the five abstracts report end-to-end savings net of miss rate and false-admit cost. As an analytical framing (the note's own, not a literature result), realized saving ≈ `per_hit_speedup × hit_rate × (1 − error_penalty)`, and the field's open frontier — vCache and the KVCache workload study — lives precisely on the two terms Folklore also lives on: raising hit rate and bounding error. Folklore's contribution (+17.5 pts cooperative correct-resolve, +5.44M tokens reused, 1.0% false-admit) is a claim about the *hit-rate × error* factors via federation, not about per-hit speedup — and it is a corpus-simulator result, not a pilot measurement.

**What the literature shows.** The five sources split into *exact/structural reuse* and *semantic reuse*, and savings magnitude tracks that split.

Exact/structural reuse holds the biggest multipliers. Prompt Cache (arXiv:2311.04934, MLSys 2024) precomputes attention states for reusable prompt segments and reports 8× (GPU) to 60× (CPU) TTFT reduction on long prompts, with no parameter changes and maintained output accuracy — but gives no hit-rate or token-reduction figures, only the latency multipliers. SGLang/RadixAttention (arXiv:2312.07104) reuses KV cache across structured calls for up to 6.4× throughput across agent control, logical reasoning, few-shot, JSON decoding, RAG, and multi-turn chat; the abstract gives only the aggregate figure, no per-task breakdown or hit rates. The KVCache workload study (arXiv:2506.02634, USENIX ATC'25) is the sobering one: from a provider's traces it finds reuse is skewed yet predictable *per request category*, single-turn reuse matters as much as multi-turn, and a *moderate* cache suffices for an ideal hit ratio — but it deliberately reports no speedup or hit-rate numbers, only that a workload-aware eviction policy helps under limited capacity.

Semantic reuse — Folklore's actual category — reports more modest, more honestly-bounded gains. GPTCache (aclanthology 2023.nlposs-1.24, EMNLP NLP-OSS 2023) claims 2–10× faster responses *when the cache is hit*, plus unquantified API-cost savings, but discloses no hit rate, false-negative rate, or threshold. vCache (arXiv:2502.03771, ICLR 2026) is the key paper: it replaces static similarity thresholds with per-prompt, online-learned thresholds and reports up to 12.5× higher cache hit rate and 26× lower error rate, *with user-defined error-rate guarantees*. The sources implicitly conflict on rigor — Prompt Cache/SGLang/GPTCache assert quality is "maintained" with no error budget, whereas vCache and the KVCache study treat the unsolved problem as exactly the *rate and correctness* of hits, not the per-hit speedup.

**How it maps to Folklore.** Folklore is semantic reuse, so vCache and GPTCache are its true comparables, not Prompt Cache/SGLang (which reuse exact tokens/KV state — a different and larger per-hit win, but one that depends on exact prompt-prefix / token overlap, so its hit conditions are narrower). This *CONFIRMS* Folklore's adopted honest framing: federated semantic-cache reuse, not cold-retrieval SOTA, bounded by retrieval precision. vCache *EXTENDS* Folklore directly: Folklore's 1.0% false-admit is the same quantity vCache turns into a *guarantee* via per-prompt online thresholds — Folklore currently reports a single observed rate, not a user-set bound, an obvious next step. The KVCache study *CHALLENGES* Folklore's compounding story by analogy: it shows reuse is skewed and a moderate cache nears the ideal hit ratio — but that result is measured on KV (exact prefix/token) reuse inside one provider's serving stack, so transferring "a moderate local cache captures most of the value" to *semantic, cross-peer* reuse is an analogy, not direct evidence; it is consistent with Folklore having retired the 9.1× boolean-model compounding figure as a simulator artifact. Critically, none of the literature validates Folklore's headline as a *compute/latency/token* saving: +5.44M tokens reused is a reuse count, +17.5 pts is a quality delta — neither is a measured wall-clock or dollar saving, and both come from the real-geometry graded simulator (`bench-compounding-real.mjs` / `-graded.mjs`: correct-resolve = energy-gate admit AND top cached doc is qrel-relevant), not a pilot. Note these are distinct from the repo's front-page boolean compounding numbers (90.2% vs 18.4%, "0% false-admit"); the honest, retrieval-grounded pair is 49.0% vs 31.5% at 1.0% false-admit.

**Open questions / the falsifier.** The sharpest unresolved question: does federation raise the realized hit rate *beyond what a tuned single-node semantic cache achieves* — is there net value in the "P2P" once you account for the KVCache study's finding that a moderate local cache nears the ideal hit ratio (granting the analogy from KV to semantic reuse)? The falsifier: run isolated-vs-cooperative with each node holding a vCache-style tuned local cache at a fixed error guarantee (e.g. 1%), measured end-to-end (latency + tokens), not simulated. If cooperative resolve does not beat isolated by a meaningful margin once the local cache is already well-tuned — or if matching the 1% false-admit forces the federated hit rate down to the isolated level — then Folklore's federation premium is a simulator artifact of an under-tuned local baseline, and the honest saving collapses to the single-node semantic-cache number the literature already reports.
