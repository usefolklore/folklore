Chapter 01 — The thesis

The cooperation web
is older than you think.

Giant platforms didn't invent the internet. They won a chapter of it. The chapter before that — the one that worked without anyone's permission — ran on peers.

2001BitTorrent
2001Kazaa
2002eMule
2005uTorrent
 platforms won the next decade
2026wellinformed

Before walled gardens won the middle chapter, the internet's first peer-to-peer generation proved humans cooperating directly move more data, faster, at a fraction of the cost of humans renting from an intermediary. The architecture worked. The platform model beat it on distribution and payment rails — not on performance, not on economics.

And the platforms are exhausting themselves now. API pricing that only goes up. Training runs burning hundreds of millions. A US administration can decide tomorrow that an AI lab is strategic infrastructure and reshape its trajectory with a phone call — and when that happens your workflow's weather changes overnight: prices hike, models deprecate, weights get silently swapped, terms tighten. The customer is never consulted.

Meanwhile ten thousand developers ask the same question ten thousand times a day. Ten thousand isolated 30-minute web searches. Same papers, same GitHub repos, same Stack Overflow threads, all re-derived alone. Each of us holds a shard of what's current — the regression we hit at 3 am and fixed, the library migration we finished Tuesday, the paper that dropped two hours ago. None of that is in any frozen-weight model because it happened after training ended. Alone, those shards die when the session closes. All of it billable. None of it accumulating.

The next web runs on cooperation. One peer answers once; the answer propagates through the network. Knowledge accumulates across the community faster than any quarterly pre-training dump can keep up. Decentralized means the knowledge can't be revoked — when a lab gets acquired, sanctioned, or reorganized, your graph doesn't care. Every piece of research done once and shared; every credential verified once and trusted; every answer compounding across everyone who shows up. Fewer tokens burned. Richer sessions. Best practices propagating in real time. Humans-for-humans, not humans-for-a-dashboard.

The open-source movement open-sourced the code. wellinformed open-sources the knowledge graph itself. That's the next step.

This is the first step.
Meet wellinformed.

Chapter 03 — The opposite shape

wellinformed is the opposite shape.

Every instance is a peer. Every peer runs the same code. Your graph lives on your CPU, your peers' graphs live on theirs, the protocol lets them share rooms without anyone brokering the exchange.

Every node is a peer.

No wellinformed server. No directory service. No signaling provider. You and your peers are the network — there's no other infrastructure to go down, get acquired, or change its pricing.

Every peer runs the same code.

MIT-licensed. The binary you run is the binary your peers run. No client/server split, no premium tier, no “cloud version.”

Research compounds across peers.

When a peer you trust has already investigated the topic, their embeddings and synthesis flow straight into your session. You inherit the finished work.

The network layer opens only when you say.

Three system rooms ship always-on. Every repo you index gets its own room automatically. Everything else is negotiable via the interactive share picker, one toggle at a time.

02
Every claim is signed at the source.

When a peer sends you an answer, their device signed it — you can verify in under 2 ms, offline, without asking any server who they are. Impersonation isn't a trust problem; it's a cryptographic one.

What v4 changed
27 ms
cached query via the native Rust CLI → daemon IPC → L1 hit. 33× the cold boot.
9.9×
indexing throughput when parallel callers coalesce into batched embeds. 2.9× on the serial path — on by default.
48×
on-disk vector compression via sign-bit-packed Matryoshka-512. −1.79 NDCG worst case across four BEIR sets.
6.29×
graph shrinkage from episodic-to-semantic consolidation: 7,002 raw session entries → 1,113 distilled memories, no content loss.
What didn't work — 13 documented null attacks. RRF sweeps, dense PRF, LLM contextualization (0.5B + 3B + 20B), cross-encoder rerankers (bge-reranker-base, InRanker-base), room routing, PPR, Jacobi preconditioning, qrel rejudges V1/V2. All measured, all nulled, all in the repo with reproduction scripts. A 20B LLM-as-judge audit (Cohen's κ=0.7053, 100% precision over 129 controls) shows the true calibrated ceiling is ~77%. We publish the failures because the hard part of retrieval is knowing what you can't claim.
Chapter 06 — Join the federation

Three system rooms
every peer advertises.
One room per repo.

No opt-in, no manual sharing. wellinformed sorts each piece of content you have into the right room automatically — a git commit, a paper, a web page, a saved note all land where they belong. The three system rooms ship always-on; every codebase you index gets its own room automatically, so queries in one project never bleed into another. Peers see what you know about tools, research, and open questions without you curating anything. Age-aware: older material is flagged so your agent knows when to refresh.
